1. The Clue Structure

Cryptic crossword clues are composed like an equation. They are a compound of (typically two) distinct elements, that independently resolve to the same answer. They contain a definition, which is typically a synonym of or description of the solution. The rest of the clue is usually wordplay, where the solver must identify an indicator (or multiple), which act as an instruction for how to manipulate the fodder, the remaining words in the clue.

There are several conventions that cryptic clues follow to make them fairer to solve, and are good to remember:

  • The definition must be either at the beginning or end of the clue.
  • Indicators cannot reference fodder that they do not neighbour directly.

2. Types of Wordplay

These are the main types of wordplay that appear in cryptic clues. The solver must work out which to apply when solving a clue based on indicators within the clue.

Anagrams

A very simple kind of wordplay, anagrams involve rearranging letters to create a new word or phrase. They are usually indicated by words suggesting change, movement, destruction, and drunkenness. It is unrealistic to memorise all possible anagram indicators, but many are synonymous within their categories.

Charades

Combining smaller words or parts to form a larger word.

Containers

Placing one word inside another.

Deletion

Removing parts of words.

Hidden

Word/s hidden within fodder.

Homophone

Words that sound the same as other words/phrases.

Letter Selection

Taking specific sections of words (first, middle, final letters etc.)

Reversal

Reversing the order of letters in a word or phrase

Other Indicators

spoonerisms, palindromes

combining indicators, juxtaposition indicators
